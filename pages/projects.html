Projects

Below is a list of some of my projects from the past few years, either from my research or from my free time. If you are interested in any or have any questions, let me know!

Optimal Particle Identification

I developed a heuristic that can be used to classify particle types (pion, kaon, proton) based off of the energy-loss readouts that they leave in the ATLAS detector at CERN following proton-proton collisions. I showed that a classifier using this heuristic outperforms the previous standard, and proved its optimality.

Read the paper at arXiv:1803.08974, and see the code here.

Quark/Gluon Regression Model

I worked with Dr. Ben Nachman to design and implement a regression model for extracting exclusive quark and gluon probability distributions from mixed particle jets resulting from proton-proton collisons measured at the ATLAS particle detector at CERN. I demonstrated the effectiveness of this method on particle-level data simulated in Pythia 8 (particle simulator) by the ATLAS Monte Carlo, running on NERSC (National Energy Research Scientific Computing Center) supercomputers.

See my presentation here.

Quantum Algorithms

While working at Rigetti Computing as a Software Engineer Intern, I helped to develop quantum algorithms using their open source platform Forest. I implemented algorithms such as Shor's Algorithm, the Duestch-Jozsa Algorithm, arbitrary state generation, and more. I also built a website to demonstrate the power of the Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm to find approximate solutions to NP-complete problems.

See my contributions at github.com/rigetticomputing/grove and github.com/rigetticomputing/pyquil.

Letter Classifier

The introduction to machine learning course at UC Berkeley hosted a Kaggle competition, in which each student in the course wrote a neural net to classify handwritten letters from the MNIST dataset. My implementation utilized ReLU activation in the hidden layers and a softmax output layer, regularized with L2 and dropout. It scored a testing accuracy 1.327% higher than that of the course staff.

Enigma

I wrote a computer simulation of the German M4 Enigma Machine used to encrypt messages during World War II. It recreates the full Enigma system used by the German Navy, consisting of eight moving rotors, two fixed rotors, and two reflectors. It implements a progressive substitution cipher, allowing for 614,175,744 unique possible encryptions for any text string.
